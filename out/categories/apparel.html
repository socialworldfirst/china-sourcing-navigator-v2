Apparel & Textile Manufacturing in China

China produced over 50% of the world's textiles and clothing in 2024. Finished garment exports alone reached $159 billion, with upstream textiles adding another $142 billion. The industry spans 210+ industrial clusters involving 200,000+ businesses across Guangdong, Zhejiang, Jiangsu, Shandong, and Fujian.

The supply chain is not centralised in one city. Raw cotton comes from Xinjiang. Fabric is woven and dyed in Shaoxing. Garments are cut and sewn in Guangzhou, Dongguan, or Hangzhou. Each stage has its own cluster of factories, and knowing which city handles which stage is the difference between paying factory price and paying platform retail.

Home to 3 of China’s top 5 clothing wholesale centres

Guangzhou is where China’s fashion supply chain begins. Shahe market alone ships 3,000+ tons of clothing daily and launches 2,000+ new styles every morning before 11am. Shisanhang — named after the original 13 trading houses from the Canton System — runs fast fashion from sketch to rack in under 72 hours. Baima serves the premium end, ISO 9001 certified, 2,000+ shops across 10 floors.

The reason this works is Zhongda — Asia’s largest fabric market, 5 minutes south. 23,000 fabric shops feed the garment factories in Haizhu and Panyu. Raw cotton from Xinjiang gets milled, dyed, cut, and sewn within a 50km radius. That vertical integration is why Guangzhou apparel consistently undercuts Hangzhou and Yiwu on the same quality tier.

One in five sweaters sold globally starts on a Dalang knitting machine

China’s knitwear supply chain is anchored in Dalang town, where 28,000 woollen textile enterprises and 200,000 workers produce 900 million sweaters a year. The yarn alone turns over 30 billion RMB annually through 2,500+ mills clustered in the same district. When global fast-fashion brands need rapid prototyping on knitted garments, Dalang’s density of specialised machinery and skilled operators means a sample can move from yarn to finished piece faster than anywhere else in the world.

Thirty minutes south, Humen town runs the distribution side: 42 billion CNY in garment output, with Fumin Fashion City housing 1,000+ companies across women’s wear, men’s wear, and children’s lines. Houjie town adds a footwear cluster of 4,000 companies. Together, Dongguan’s three towns form a triangle that handles knitwear production, fashion wholesale, and shoe manufacturing without leaving one municipality.

China’s fast-fashion supply chain starts at Sijiqing market before the sun comes up

Hangzhou’s Sijiqing wholesale market — ‘Garment First Street of China’ — opens at 5am and runs across 50,000 square metres with 3,000+ retail spaces, 1,200+ manufacturers, and 900+ branded lines. It is the hub that feeds China’s wanghong (influencer) fashion economy: 70% of the country’s internet-famous clothing stores are based in Hangzhou, and 90% of them source from Sijiqing. As Alibaba’s home city, Hangzhou’s e-commerce infrastructure is unmatched.

The city’s silk heritage adds a premium layer. Chinese Silk City on Xinhua Road houses 600+ silk enterprises, offering pure silk garments, scarves, and ties that trade on centuries of Hangzhou’s reputation as the silk capital. For apparel buyers targeting women’s fashion or direct-to-consumer e-commerce brands, Hangzhou offers something Guangzhou does not: a design-led ecosystem where trend speed meets digital distribution.

One in five pairs of sneakers on the planet is stitched together in Chendai town

The global sportswear supply chain has a nerve centre in Jinjiang. Chendai town alone hosts 7,000+ shoe manufacturers turning out over 1 billion pairs a year, with output value exceeding 50 billion CNY. This is where Anta (62.4 billion RMB revenue), Xtep, Peak, and 361 Degrees were born and still produce. The local saying — ‘every shoe component within 30 minutes’ — captures the density.

Yinglin town, also in Jinjiang, covers the apparel side with 1,000+ garment enterprises producing casual wear, swimwear, and athletic apparel worth 20+ billion CNY annually. Nearby Shishi adds menswear wholesale with 600,000 sqm of market space and 4,782 shops.

China’s fashion design hub and the gateway for export-grade apparel

Shanghai is where Chinese fashion meets international standards. The city hosts Shanghai Fashion Week twice a year with 100+ runway shows and 1,000+ brands, plus the MODE Shanghai trade show that connects Chinese manufacturers with global buyers. Qipu Road, Shanghai’s largest apparel wholesale market, stretches 680 metres with 10+ buildings and over a century of trading history.

For importers, Shanghai’s value is design, branding, and trade infrastructure rather than factory floors. The South Bund Fabric Market offers custom tailoring at a third of European bespoke prices. The city’s port, Yangshan Deep Water Port, handles more container volume than any port in the world. Apparel designed or sampled in Shanghai typically gets manufactured in Guangzhou or Dongguan, then ships from Shanghai.

How to Source Apparel from China

Four ways to find apparel suppliers. Most successful importers use multiple approaches at different stages.

Search on 1688

1688 is Alibaba's domestic B2B marketplace — where Chinese factories trade with each other. For apparel, that means factory-gate pricing on garments, fabrics, and trims that Alibaba.com doesn't surface. Start at fuzhuang.1688.com, the dedicated apparel channel.

How to find apparel suppliers on 1688

1
Search in Chinese

Use Chinese product terms like 广州女装 (Guangzhou women’s clothing), 牛仔裤批发 (jeans wholesale), or 针织衫批发 (knitwear wholesale).

2
Filter by apparel hubs

Set location to 广州 (Guangzhou), 杭州 (Hangzhou), or 东莞 (Dongguan). Use fuzhuang.1688.com as your entry point.

3
Check factory badges

Look for 超级工厂 (Super Factory) and 实力商家 (Power Merchant). Check repeat buyer rate (回头率) — 20%+ is a good sign.

4
Verify before paying

Cross-check the business registration. Use WorldFirst to pay in RMB directly to the supplier’s 1688/Alipay account.
